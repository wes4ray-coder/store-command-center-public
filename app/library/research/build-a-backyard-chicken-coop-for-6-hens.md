# Build a backyard chicken coop for 6 hens

*Research report by Newton — the Research Lab, 2026-07-18.*

## Overview
This project is the construction of a functional, predator-proof backyard chicken coop designed specifically for a flock of six hens. The primary goal is to create a durable, low-cost shelter that maximizes space efficiency while remaining accessible for a DIY builder using basic tools. Key decisions include using pressure-treated lumber for ground contact to prevent rot, implementing a "hardware cloth" mesh system for predator security, and prioritizing easy-clean waste management to ensure long-term hygiene.

## Step-by-step guide
1.  **Site Preparation**: Level the ground in your backyard. Clear debris and lay down a gravel or wood chip base to improve drainage and reduce mud.
2.  **Foundation and Floor Framing**: Build a rectangular base frame using pressure-treated 2x4 lumber. Secure the frame to the ground using heavy-duty stakes or a concrete pad. Lay 3/4" exterior-grade plywood over the frame to form the floor.
3.  **Wall Framing**: Construct four wall frames using 2x4 studs. Ensure you include openings for a large side window (ventilation), a human-sized door for cleaning, and a smaller "pop door" for the hens.
4.  **Roof Framing**: Build a simple A-frame roof structure. Use rafters to create a peak, allowing for water runoff.
5.  **Sheathing and Siding**: Attach exterior siding (T1-11 siding or plywood) to the wall frames. Install roofing material (corrugated metal or asphalt shingles) over the rafters.
6.  **Predator-Proofing**: Cover all windows and openings with 1/2-inch galvanized hardware cloth. Secure it to the frames using heavy-duty staples or screws.
7.  **Interior Amenities Installation**:
    *   **Roosts**: Mount 2x4 boards (flat side up) along the interior walls, spaced about 12 inches apart.
    *   **Nesting Boxes**: Build a partitioned box (at least 12"x12" per hen) on the side of the coop for egg-laying.
    *   **Feeders/Waterers**: Mount these at the height of the hens to keep them off the floor.
8.  **Finishing Touches**: Apply a non-toxic exterior stain or paint to protect the wood from weather. Install heavy-duty latches on all doors.

## Safety notes
*   **Power Tool Safety**: Always wear safety goggles and gloves when using drills or saws. Ensure the work area is clear of tripping hazards.
*   **Ammonia Levels**: Ensure the coop has cross-ventilation. High ammonia levels from waste can cause respiratory issues for hens and humans.
*   **Secure Latching**: Use "carabiner-style" or spring-loaded latches. Predators (like raccoons) are intelligent enough to open simple sliding bolts.
*   **Lifting**: Use proper lifting techniques when moving heavy plywood sheets or lumber to avoid back strain.

## Tips & pitfalls
*   **The "Raccoon Gap"**: A common pitfall is leaving gaps larger than 1 inch. If a predator can fit its head through, it can get the rest of its body through. Always use hardware cloth, not chicken wire (which is too flimsy).
*   **Height Matters**: Build the coop tall enough for you to walk into easily. This makes cleaning significantly easier and reduces the risk of back injury.
*   **Roost Height**: Place roosts high up to keep the "sleeping area" dry and away from floor waste.
*   **Waste Management**: Use a "deep litter" method (adding wood shavings to the floor) to reduce the frequency of cleaning needed.
